Matthew 1:2

Contextual Background

King of the Jews

Once being acquainted with the comment on verse one, we can agree that these Magi chose the worst possible title of the Messiah to break the news to herod: βασιλεὺς τῶν Ἰουδαίων ("King of the Jews.")

His entire life Herod has been fighting for recognition from the Jews not only as their king, but also as a Jew. He has achieved the first but he has not had the same luck withe the second one. In fact they never will come to recognize him as a Jew 1.
Now he has to face reality. His greatest fear has come true: the Jew of Jews has been born. Someone from the lineage of David with all the rights to the throne and long-awaited by all Jews. This Messiah is supposed to be the greatest political figure and war general 2 who would finally overthrow the yoke of the Romans and reestablish David's Kingdom in Jerusalem. (2samuel 7:12-13; 1 Chronicles 17:10-14; Isaiah 2:1-4; Isaiah 16:5; Daniel 2:44; Daniel 7:27; Mica 41-5; Jeremiah 23:51). We can only imagine the fear and anxiety that took Herod when hearing the news.

it is important to note that the tile "King of the Jews" will never be used in a genuine way in the rest of the Gospels by a Jew, and perhaps by a gentile either, since the only other person who might be argued to use it genuinely is Pilate, and it is hard to conclude whether he presented Him as King of the Jews in a mocking way or whether he was actually serious (John 19:19; John 19:14; John 18:39).

However, even if we give Pilate the benefit of doubt and take the position that he was indeed being genuine, he would have been the only other individual in the pages of the Gospels using the term in a serious manner. Therefore, out of the (at least) 15 times that King of the Jews appears in the Bible only 2 times (John 19:21; Luke 23:37) is used by Jews. All other 13 times is used by gentiles. Every time the title is used by the Jews is used as a sign of mockery or disdain, and only 1 of 13 times used by a gentile we can be sure is used seriously (Matthew 2:2).

When Jesus is referred to as King in a serious manner by a Jew, the more common Jewish term "king of Israel" is used (Matthew 27:42; Mark 15:32). Therefore we see this title occurring on many occasions but only during two events of His life: His birth and His death. An even more interesting detail is that these Magi did not come to see just a baby who would one day become King of the Jews, but one who τεχθεὶς (was born) King of the Jews. Nobody is born a king. A newborn might come to this world as an heir to his father's, throne, but not as an already established king. For the firs time in the history of the world, a baby was born a king, or better said the King, and the Magi knew this.

He was born King of the Jews because this was Jehovah of the Old Testament, who was coming into the world but not into existence.
Before laying in a manger, He already sat on a throne (psalm to 3:19). Before he was surrounded by a group of Magi, He already was surrounded by a host of angels 1 Kings 22:19. Before He was wrapped in swaddling clothes, His robe already filled the Temple (Isaiah 66:1). Before Magi loaded donkeys and camels with gold and traveled across sandy roads, He was already making highways, bridges, and foundations with it (Revelation 21:21). Before the Magi discovered the constellations in the sky He was already walking over them. (Amos 4:13)

The revelation of the Magi went far beyond the fact that He was a king, they knew He was the King. Because He was born king, He hung on the cross as King. Because He was born King Pilate had to write: I have written what I have written (John 19:22)

The star: what was it?

These great group of astrologers leave no room for doubt as to what was the catalyzer for their journey: a star. Nevertheless, we can deduct that this star was not just the common kind that we can appreciate on about every clear night. This was not just another star among the thousands. It is most certain that whatever phenomena they witnessed had to be something unprecedented for multiple reasons:

  1. These men were no common personalities. They were elite Persian 3 or Babylonian 4 scholars who's ancestors had been studying the heavenly bodies for thousands of years. It is quite clear that whatever astronomy phenomena sent them on their journey was not something small or something they had seen before.
  2. The road to Jerusalem from Babylon was not a short trip, neither was it easy. The journey was at least 550 miles across a quite difficult and sandy road. That is assuming that they came from Babylon. If thy came from persia as some argue, their trip was almost twice as long an much more difficult 5
  3. Another reason to conclude that the star was in reality something much bigger and amazing than that is the fact that these Magi came to bring the news not to the pharisees or the teachers of the Law, but to Herod. Anyone with some common sense in those days knew that you don't approach a king to let him know he is about to be replaced. These Magi were bringing the news at the potential cost of their lives.
So what was it that these men saw in reality? The answer is a difficult one, and one that we will never know with 100% certainty. At least not on this side of heaven. The theories range from a guiding angel to comets, nova stars, steroids, and more.
Multiple factors contribute to the difficulty of the text, for example the lack of description about the object and the meaning of the word ἀστέρα (star). Although the term is mainly used to refer to the relative small and shiny objects we see at night, the word is also used in the Scriptures as to mean planets. An example of this is Jude 1:13. He uses the phrase ἀστέρες πλανῆται which transliterated is asteres planētai (stars planets). The word πλανῆται (planetai) is a noun meaning wanderer. Therefore, Jude is differentiating from the other kind of stars, the relatively "fixed stars" (when compared to the earth). It is possible, therefore, that what he has in mind here is the idea of a planet rather than a star. This idea is not hard to reconcile since even in our present day the Sun, the Earth, and most heavenly bodies can be referred to as stars.

Multiple possibilities are suggested for explaining the star of Bethlehem:

  1. Planet Venus
  2. Halley's Comet
  3. Fireball or Meteor Shower
  4. A Supernova star
  5. Triple planet conjunction
  6. A sequence of many events
Venus

The word Venus comes from the Roman goddess of love but the planet was discovered centuries before Roman civilization 6 The first observation of Venus can be dated to 1700 B. C. by an Assyrian astronomer. We know from history that Babylonians knew the planet Venus as Istar or Ishtar who was the mother of the Gods. 7 and the personi­fication of Everything that was attractive in a woman.

Alexander Hislop makes the connection of the Roman Venus with the Greek Aphrodite, the Babylonian Semiramis, and the Asherah, Ashtoreth, or Astarte of the Canaanites8, who's worship Solomon endorsed and promoted (2 Kings 23:13).

Venus can be seen around Christmas time most. years, and when it is high in the sky it shines quit brightly. 9. Because it is seen around Christmas time and it can outshine the other stars when positioned in the right place, it has taken a popular place among the candidates for the star of Bethlehem. However, as stated before, The Magi, being acquainted with this star goddess, Venus would have not been surprised by such event. They had been observing it for centuries, it is hard to imagine that such common event would send them on a voyage so long and onerous.

Halley's Comet.

The second theory most popular is that the star of Bethlehem was actually Halley's comet. 10 The Halley commit is what astrologers call a "periodic comet" which simply means that it has an orbit of less than 200 years. The first recorded appearance of Halley's comet was in £40 B.C. and it is said to have been visible 28 times since then 11.

The Comet is named after the English astronomer Edmond Halley, who in 1705 discovered that this comet, which had been observed before, had a consistent appearance of 76.5 years, thus he successfully predicted the next appearance in 1758, since it had last happened in 16822.

If the comet made an appearance in 1758 and 1682, we can can count backwards in intervals of -76.5. If we do this, we will find out that at interval 23 an appearance of the comet happens in year 0; very close to the approximated date of Christ's birth. However, recent astronomers have found out that the interval of the comet was not fixed, as Halley extrapolated. From ancient chinese records, it is assumed that a comet documented in 12 B.C. was indeed Halley's comet, which would throw off Halley's math and therefore eradicate the theory that the comet happened in year zero. This, in consequence discards the theory of Halley's comet as a possible answer for the star of Bethlehem from the list.

Fireball or Meteor Shower

Another possibility postulated to fill the role of this phenomena has been a Fireball or a Meteor Shower. A fireball can actually surpass the brightness of the Moon in rare occasions, which would make for an spectacular event; Meteor showers, on the other hand, have been known by the Chinese and Babylonian Civilizations for more than 2000 years 12 and perhaps would not have caused as much of an amaze, but if this was at just a little more amazing than the ordinary, and if the direction it showered on was towards Jerusalem, it could have been interpreted by the Magi as a series os small signs pointing them towards the Holy Land.

A meteor or a meteor shower, however, is also a very unlikely answer, since these phenomenon usually last less than a second, yet, the Star the Magi observed went ahead of them (Matthew 2:9). Not only that, but the Magi would have needed the repetition of the same event, as the star which they saw guided them first to Jerusalem (west of Babylon) but then also towards Bethlehem (South of Jerusalem), something that adds to the unlikeliness of the event, since quite usually none of these occur on a close sequence.

A Supernova

A supernova is the death of a star. I will not go into the specific details of the event for two reasons: one, it would make for a lengthy read, and two and most importantly, the chemical and physical reactions that take place when a star dies are far beyond my knowledge. Suffice it to say that the death of a star is an incredibly violent activity, but at the same time it makes for a beautiful spectacle. The supernova ends in a massive explosion. In consequence the energy within the star is released. Supernovas are believed to be the second greatest generators of energy in the universe 13.

The light produced by a supernova can remain visible for up to 9 months, which satisfies the issue of the journey time frame, since, as we have mentioned before, it probably took the Magi no longer than 4 months if they came from Babylon. On the other hand, according to astrological records, there has not been a Supernova that has occurred close enough to the time of Birth of our Lord, and thus, this option is typically also ruled out.

Triple planet conjunction

A planet conduction occurs when one astronomical object pass either north or South of another one producing the illusion, as seen from earth, that they are coming together, yet, most times this astronomical bodies can be millions of miles away from each other. This astronomical bodies can include asteroids, moons, planets, and stars.

A popular conduction is the conjunction between Mars and Jupiter or Mars an Saturn that happens approximately every 2 years 14.

A triple conjunction is a little different than a normal conjunction and it happens when 2 astronomical bodies come together and separate, 3 times, as appearing to "dance" with each other. This is a very rare event and not many are documented in history. This process is not a one day process, however, it can take months for a triple conjunction to finish, as each one of the conjunctions, or "crossings of the planets", are spread out and not immediately consecutive to each other. from the year 1000. B.C. to 7 A.D. only a total of 7 triple conjunctions are recorded. This number includes only the conjunctions visible to the naked eye, of course, taking into consideration that there was no other means of observing this phenomena in the ancient world. A conjunction that is visible to the naked eye is something that is possible for those planets in our orbit only.

Special interest is paid to the one occurring in 7. B.C. when considering candidates for the star of Bethlehem. This was a triple conjunction between Jupiter and Saturn in the constellation of pisces, a constellation usually associated with the Jews. astrological meaning.

The conduction spread over a period of seven months, with the first conduction happening in May 23rd, the second in October 5th and the last one in December 5th - 8th 15. Some believe that even though this happened at least a couple years before the birth of our Lord, the Magi took this as the first sign of a series that would follow and they kept awaiting for further instructions in the skies before making their trip. Others are not so optimistic about this explanation, for it is true that in ancient Babylon Saturn was regarded as a an evil planet 16, thus this astronomical event would not have had a positive astrological meaning, and if there was not a positive astronomical meaning there is no possibility for a strong argument that supports that this was the catalyzer of their trip.

A sequence of many events

Mark R. Kidger, researcher of astrophysics, offers a different solution. Rather than paying attention to a single event, he states that the phenomena that sent the Magi on their journey was not the occurrence of a single phenomena but a compilation of astrologically meaningful events. He proposes the following sequence of events: 17

  1. Triple conduction of Jupiter and saturn in 7 B.C.
  2. The planetary amassing of Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn in 6. B.C.
  3. Pairing of Jupiter in Pisces in 5 B.C.
  4. A Nova star recorded in ancient Chinese records during 5 or 4 B.C
1. Triple conduction of Jupiter and saturn in 7 B.C.

The first case on the sequence of events that Kidger pays attention to is the triple conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn, which as mentioned above, occurred in Pisces, the constellation of the Jews, This would have provoked special interest on these Babylonian/ Persian astrologers /astronomers. Because the conjunction involved Jupiter, the planet of the king, 18 and Saturn, the evil planet, they would assume that there was a confrontation between a king and evil, 19, and from then on they kept watch with keen attention to the heavens for more signs.

2. The planetary amassing of Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn in 6. B.C.

The next sign in this sequence, says kiddger, was an amassing of the planets Jupiter, Saturn, and Mars, that followed the triple conjunction. This happened in February 6 B.C., also in the constellations of Pisces.

3. Pairing of Jupiter in Pisces in 5 B.C.

The third sign came in February 20, 5 B.C. when the moon passed very closely to Jupiter. With this many sequential events happening in Pisces, the Maggi would have extrapolated that something was about to happen in Judea. The speculation from the Magi that Kidger proposes is that Jupiter, the Benevolent king would crush Saturn, the evil one, by the sword, (Since mars was associated with war and Blood), in Judea. This no doubt, would recall to their mind an old passage from Balaam, probably passed down to them by Daniel, (Numbers 24:17).

4. A Nova star recorded in ancient Chinese records during 5 or 4 B.C

In the Spring of 5 or 4 B. C. the chinese recorded an object in the sky that shone brightly for 70 days, this was also found in ancient Korean Records. Astronomers today agree that this phenomena was most probably a supernova. After witnessing the last three major astrological / astronomical events, the Nova was the sign that sealed the start of their journey. A very favorable argument to this event is that it happened during the birth year of the Lord Jesus Christ, as extrapolated by some historians.

Conclusion

Of course, however convening any of these theories sound, we cannot prove their veracity nor accuracy. Regardless of how many more theories come up throughout the years, they will not cease to be only that, theories. Nevertheless, even when we cannot know with certainty whether the star was a miracle or an astronomical occurrence, we can be confident that this event was a true event, and that if God has the power to call stars into instance (Genesis 1:16) and to call then by name (Psalm 147:4), He surely has the power to hang a new one to signal His coming to earth if He chooses so. This idea is hard to understand for the enemies of God's word, since the carnal man cannot know the things of the Spirit (2 Cor. 2:14)

Critics of the Star of Bethlehem deny the appearance of a star and claim it was a made up tale by Matthew, and some even go as far as to deny the coming of the Magi to see Christ 20. Critics, like R. M. Jenkins, claim that the story of Matthew was made up to convince Jewish readers that Jesus was indeed the Messiah, and to keep up with the custom of the time, in which astronomical events were interpreted to signal the birth and death of rulers 21.

The problem of the critic, however, is not the lack of evidence, but the lack of faith. Their problem is not that they know much about a specific scientific field that rules miracles out, but rather that they do not know the God who work miracles in.

The fact that there is not enough evidence that the star indeed was a real occurrence, does not mean it was not. Just because something is not testable or repeatable does not follow it is not possible. It is unscientific to say that something is impossible only because it is not natural or testable. Critics of miracles often use this argument ignoring that their very argument rules out the most cherished foundations of science as the Big bang or the theory of evolution, for neither of those are natural nor can be tested. Hence, only because a miracle is not scientific, it does not follow is not possible.

As a matter of fact, it is not the task of science to deal with the supernatural. There are at least four types of questions science cannot answer:

  • Morality and Ethics: What's good vs bad.
  • Aestheticism: what's beautiful vs ugly.
  • Questions of application: how to use your scientific knowledge.
  • Supernatural Explanations: Does God exist? Do miracles happen? Science Cannot explain this.

Not matter how much the critic tries to disprove Biblical truths, their best explanations will never go beyond the status of being a theory. As for us, Children of God and worshipers of Jesus Christ, should not loose sleep over small details as this. We hold fast to the truth that Christ indeed came, died, and resurrected regardless of what the sign that brought the Magi around was.

God will meet the hungry soul regardless of the circumstances

I think it has been the problem of any thinking mind to question what would happen to those who have never hear the gospel? What about those poor tribes living under a hoot and eating their neighboring fellows? Is it just for God to condemn them to Hell simply because they have not heard the Gospel? Is not this a fault of the church rather than of these poor people? How can a loving and just God do this?

The first thing we must remember is that before the world had borders and gaps, God had already designed the specific place and era where you and I would be born (Act.`17 26:). The second fact is that He is not limited by geographical circumstances. Could He have created the earth and those living in it, and yet fail to deliver to them the only message of hope? Of course not. He will reveal Himself to whomever He wants to reveal Himself to (Exo. 33:19). And His desire is that everyone turns to Him (Ezekiel 18:32).

Therefore, the question is, can people who have never known Jesus Christ, and as a result have not accepted Him as Lord, be saved? The answer is a big NO! There is no salvation outside Jesus Christ (Jhn. 14:6; Acts 4:12;).

It does not matter whether a person has heard the gospel or not, if there is no heaven without a relationship with Christ. Hence, should we feel bad about the poor man/ woman in the middle of nowhere who weeps and seeks unendingly for God but has no one to preach to him/ her? The truth is that feeling sorry for such person would feel as feeling sorry for a fictitious character of a comics book because that person does not exist. There is not a person in the world that wants to know God that God will not reveal Himself to.

Whether is through a missionary or through a dream or vision, God will not fail to His Word (Mat. 7:8). Every human in the world has been given enough light to be enticed to seek God (Romans 1:18-20; Psa. 19:1). Nature is God's evangelist to the world. Therefore, even not all have heard about Jesus Christ, everyone has heard and/ or seen about God. Not everyone has been given the light of the Gospel, but everyone has been given the light of the sun.

My main concern, however, is not about the fellow out there in the jungle who have never known about Christ. My concern is for those who have heard of Him and rejected Him. They are the ones in trouble, for we will be rewarded according to the amount of light given to us (Luk. 12:48; Luke 10:13-14).

My concern is not about the person under a hoot, my concern is about the person who sees John 3:16 in every corner and God loves you in every billboard and still rejects God. Not for those who don't know what a bible is, but for those who know it from Genesis to Revelation and yet live as if they did not. Those who know what sin is and still decide to indulge on it. if this you, how frightening is to be you! If I was a cannibal in the jungle I would be sorry for you.

They talked to Herod straight to the face and told him there was a new King, one that was worthy of worship. Whatever God is asking you to do that you think is too hard, my friend, will never be as hard as paying for the mistake of your disobedience.
