Matthew 1:2

Contextual Background

King of the Jews

Once being acquainted with the comment on verse one, we can agree that these Magi chose the worst possible title of the Messiah to break the news to herod: βασιλεὺς τῶν Ἰουδαίων ("king of the Jews.")

His entire life Herod has been fighting for recognition from the Jews not only as their king, but also as a Jew. He has achieved the first but he has not had the same luck withe the second one. In fact they never will come to recognize him as a Jew {1}.
Now he has to face reality. His greatest fear has come true: the Jew of Jews has been born. Someone from the lineage of David with all the rights to the throne and long-awaited by all Jews. This Messiah is supposed to be the greatest political figure and war general {2} who would finally overthrow the yoke of the Romans and reestablish David's Kingdom in Jerusalem. (2samuel 7:12-13; 1 Chronicles 17:10-14; Isaiah 2:1-4; Isaiah 16:5; Daniel 2:44; Daniel 7:27; Mica 41-5; Jeremiah 23:51). We can only imagine the fear and anxiety that took Herod when hearing the news.

it is important to note that the tile "king of the Jews" will never be used in a genuine way in the rest of the Gospels by a Jew, and perhaps by a gentile either, since the only other person who might be argued to use it genuinely is Pilate, and it is hard to conclude whether he presented Him as king of the Jews in a mocking way or whether he was actually serious (John 19:19; John 19:14; John 18:39).

However, even if we give Pilate the benefit of doubt and take the position that he was indeed being genuine, he would have been the only other individual in the pages of the Gospels using the term in a serious manner. Therefore, out of the (at least) 15 times that King of the Jews appears in the Bible only 2 times (John 19:21; Luke 23:37) is used by Jews. All other 13 times is used by gentiles. Every time the title is used by the Jews is used as a sign of mockery or disdain, and only 1 of 13 times used by a gentile we can be sure is used seriously (Matthew 2:2).

When Jesus is referred to as King in a serious manner by a Jew, the more common Jewish term "king of Israel" is used (Matthew 27:42; Mark 15:32). Therefore we see this title occurring on many occasions but only during two events of His life: His birth and His death. An even more interesting detail is that these Magi did not come to see just a baby who would one day become King of the Jews, but one who τεχθεὶς (was born) king of the Jews. Nobody is born a king. A newborn might come to this world as an heir to his father's, throne, but not as an already established king. For the firs time in the history of the world, a baby was born a king, or better said the King, and the Magi knew this.

He was born king of the Jews because this was Jehovah of the Old Testament, who was coming into the world but not into existence.
Before laying in a manger, He already sat on a throne (psalm to 3:19). Before he was surrounded by a group of Magi, He already was surrounded by a host of angels 1 Kings 22:19. Before He was wrapped in swaddling clothes, His robe already filled the Temple (Isaiah 66:1). Before Magi loaded donkeys and camels with gold and traveled across sandy roads, He was already making highways, bridges, and foundations with it (Revelation 21:21). Before the Magi discovered the constellations in the sky He was already walking over them. (Amos 4:13)

The revelation of the Magi went far beyond the fact that He was a king, they knew He was the King. Because He was born king, He hung on the cross as King. Because He was born King Pilate had to write: I have written what I have written (John 19:22)

The star: what was it?

These great group of astrologers leave no room for doubt as to what was the catalyzer for their journey: a star. Nevertheless, we can deduct that this star was not just the common kind that we can appreciate on about every clear night. This was not just another star among the thousands. It is most certain that whatever phenomena they witnessed had to be something unprecedented for multiple reasons:

  1. These men were no common personalities. They were elite Persian {3} or Babylonian {4} scholars who's ancestors had been studying the heavenly bodies for thousands of years. It is quite clear that whatever astronomy phenomena sent them on their journey was not something small or something they had seen before.
  2. The road to Jerusalem from Babylon was not a short trip, neither was it easy. The journey was at least 550 miles across a quite difficult and sandy road. That is assuming that they came from Babylon. If thy came from persia as some argue, their trip was almost twice as long an much more difficult {5}
  3. Another reason to conclude that the star was in reality something much bigger and amazing than that is the fact that these Magi came to bring the news not to the pharisees or the teachers of the Law, but to Herod. Anyone with some common sense in those days knew that you don't approach a king to let him know he is about to be replaced. These Magi were bringing the news at the potential cost of their lives.
So what was it that these men saw in reality? The answer is a difficult one, and one that we will never know with 100% certainty. At least not on this side of heaven. The theories range from a guiding angel to comets, nova stars, steroids, and more.
Multiple factors contribute to the difficulty of the text, for example the lack of description about the object and the meaning of the word ἀστέρα (star). Although the term is mainly used to refer to the relative small and shiny object we see at night, the word is also used in the Scriptures as to mean planets. An example of this is Jude 1:13. He uses the phrase ἀστέρες πλανῆται which transliterated is asteres planētai (stars planets). The word πλανῆται (planetai) is a noun meaning wanderer. Therefore Jude is making difference from the other kind of stars, the relatively "fixed stars" (when compared to the earth). It is possible, therefore, that what he has in mind here is the idea of a planet rather than a star. This idea is not hard to reconcile since even in our present day the Sun, the Earth, and most heavenly bodies can be referred to as stars. Mark Kidger, a Researcher of astrophysics, has done a great research in his book "Star of Bethlehem" about the topic.

Kidger suggests several possibilities for the star of Bethlehem:

  1. Halley's Comet
  2. Fireball or Meteor Shower
  3. a Supernova star
  4. triple planet conjunctions
  5. or the combination of many of these
Venus

The word Venus comes from the Roman goddess of love but the planet was discovered centuries before Roman civilization {6} The first observation of Venus can be dated to 1700 B. C. by an Assyrian astronomer. We know from history that Babylonians knew the planet Venus as Istar or Ishtar who was the mother of the Gods. {7} and the personi­fication of Everything that was attractive in a woman.

Alexander Hislop makes the connection of the Roman Venus withe Greek Aphrodite, the Babylonian Semiramis, and the Asherah, Ashtoreth, or Astarte of the Canaanites{8}, who's worship Salomon endorsed and promoted (2 Kings 23:13).

Venus can be seen around Christmas time most. years, and when it is high in the sky it shines quit brightly. {9}. Because it is seen around Christmas time and it can outshine the other stars when positioned in the right place, it has taken a popular place among the candidates for the star of Bethlehem. However, as stated before, The Magi, being acquainted with this star goddess, Venus would have not been surprised by such event. They had been observing it for centuries, it is hard to imagine that such common event would send them on a voyage so long and onerous).
